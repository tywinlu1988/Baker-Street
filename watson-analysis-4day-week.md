# Watson's Analysis: Adopt a 4-Day Workweek?
## Problem: 30-person SaaS, $5M ARR, mostly senior engineers, no external deadlines

---

### Core Argument

Common sense says: yes, adopt it -- but with eyes wide open about what it will cost and what it will fix. The evidence from the UK trial (61 companies, 92% continued after the trial) is strong that most organisations do not regret the switch. Revenue grew 1.4% on average during the trial, which tells me it is not a financial gamble for a healthy business. The 65% drop in sick days and 57% drop in turnover from the reduced-hours model (32 hours) are, frankly, the kind of numbers that would make any sensible business owner sit up. For a 30-person shop of senior engineers who already self-manage, the question is not "can we do this?" but "what are we hoping to get out of it?" If the goal is retaining good people and keeping them healthy, the evidence says it works. If the goal is cutting costs or magically boosting output, that is a different conversation entirely.

---

### Key Observations

- **Your revenue per employee is $167K, versus a $200K+ SaaS benchmark.** That is below where a healthy SaaS company typically sits. The ordinary observer reads this and thinks: you are already lean on headcount productivity. Cutting hours without a plan to adjust scope or process could squeeze margins further, not improve them.

- **Your people are senior engineers with no external deadlines.** This is the most important detail in the problem statement. Senior engineers are not clock-watchers. They do their best work when they have autonomy and uninterrupted focus time. A 32-hour week for this profile is less a "reduction" and more of an alignment with how they already work -- deep work in bursts, meetings compressed, admin minimised. Many senior engineers already accomplish in four days what others do in five.

- **The UK trial showed revenue grew 1.4% during the transition.** That is modest but it is growth, not decline. For a $5M ARR company, 1.4% is $70K. Not life-changing, but it refutes the fear that revenue collapses when hours drop.

- **The 57% reduction in turnover is the headline number for a 30-person company.** Replacing senior engineers in a SaaS company costs 6-9 months of salary each (recruiting, interviewing, ramp time). If the 4-day week keeps even one or two senior engineers from leaving in the next year, it has paid for itself many times over.

- **"No external deadlines" cuts both ways.** It means you have flexibility to experiment, which is good. But it also means there is no external forcing function to ensure the 4-day week actually produces efficiency gains. Some teams use the constraint of fewer hours to cut waste ruthlessly. Without deadlines, your team might just spread five days of work into four days at the same pace and lose the productivity benefit.

---

### Evidence Chain

I draw on two decades of watching professional services and software teams. Time and again, the teams that succeed with reduced hours share three traits: (1) strong measurement of output, not hours; (2) a clear definition of what "done" looks like each week; and (3) leadership that models the behaviour. The UK trial results (Fact Base) confirm what I have observed in practice -- the companies that try it mostly stick with it, and they do not see revenue declines.

The specific comparator I keep coming back to is the $167K revenue per employee. A healthy SaaS benchmark is $200K+ per head. If you were at $250K per head, I would say the 4-day week is a no-brainer retention perk. At $167K, you have less margin for error. The question becomes: will the 4-day week improve productivity enough to close that gap, or will it widen it?

The turnover reduction (57%) is the strongest practical argument. In a 30-person company of senior engineers, each departure is a crisis. A senior engineer who leaves takes months of institutional knowledge, client relationships, and codebase familiarity. If the 4-day week meaningfully reduces that risk, it is worth doing for that reason alone.

---

### Explicit Assumptions

- **Senior engineers value autonomy over income at this stage of their careers.** This is an assumption based on how knowledge workers behave in practice. They trade higher-paying, higher-pressure roles for more control over their time. A 4-day week reinforces the autonomy that keeps them engaged. I could be wrong: some may prefer the extra day to freelance, or may see it as a signal the company is struggling.

- **The $5M ARR is stable and growing enough to absorb a transition period.** I assume a company at this revenue level has product-market fit and recurring revenue that does not vanish when the office goes dark on Fridays. The 1.4% revenue growth during the UK trial supports this assumption, but every business is different.

- **Your measurement culture is mature enough to manage by output, not hours.** This is the biggest assumption. A 30-person startup may or may not have clear KPIs per engineer. If you cannot measure what each person produces, a 4-day week becomes a leap of faith.

- **The SaaS benchmark of $200K+ revenue per employee is applicable to your specific vertical and pricing model.** Industry averages are useful but not prescriptive. Your $167K may reflect your pricing tier, not inefficiency.

---

### Blind Spot Acknowledgment

I have a bias toward practical, proven approaches, and the UK trial data is compelling. But I may be underestimating two things.

First, the UK trial participants were self-selecting -- companies that volunteered to try a 4-day week were probably already well-managed and motivated to make it work. The 92% continuation rate may not replicate in a company that is less organised or that adopts the policy for the wrong reasons (e.g., to cut costs rather than to improve quality of life).

Second, I may be missing a counter-intuitive truth that Holmes would spot immediately: "no external deadlines" combined with reduced hours could produce a dangerous equilibrium where the team coasts. Without the pressure of client deliverables or hard launch dates, a 4-day week could become a 4-day week of 6-hour days with no urgency. The UK trial results show productivity held steady, but those companies had external stakeholders. Your circumstances are different.

A third blind spot: the trial data comes mostly from 2022, during a post-pandemic labour market where retention was a top priority. In a tighter market (2026), the calculus may be different. The 65% sick day reduction is impressive, but if your team already has low sick days (senior engineers tend to be healthier and more established), the absolute gain may be smaller than the percentage suggests.

---

### Practical Checklist

*To adopt or not? A common-sense decision guide.*

**Before committing, verify these three things:**
1. Can you measure output per engineer today? If not, build that first.
2. Is your $167K revenue per employee trending up or down? Up = proceed; down = fix the fundamentals first.
3. What are your current turnover and sick day rates? If low, the headline gains from the trial may not apply.

**If you proceed, do it this way:**
- Trial the reduced-hours model (32 hours, not compressed 4x10) -- the evidence base is stronger for this approach.
- Run an 8-week pilot with clear before/after metrics: output, revenue per employee, sick days, and an anonymous team sentiment survey.
- Define which hours drop. Friday afternoon is the most common choice, but for senior engineers who value deep work, cutting meeting blocks may be more impactful than cutting a full day.

**One thing I would not do:**
- Do not frame this as a cost-saving measure. The evidence does not support that. Frame it as a retention and wellbeing investment, which the evidence does support.
