# The Baker Street Deduction: A 4-Day Workweek for Your SaaS Company

## Preamble: The Available Evidence

I have been furnished with a Shared Fact Base containing five verified claims from reputable sources. I have attempted to gather supplementary evidence via web investigation, but connectivity constraints limit me to this base. I shall proceed with what is known, flagging all gaps explicitly.

## Core Argument

Your 25-person SaaS company *should* adopt a 4-day workweek — but only as a **structured, time-boxed trial with explicit productivity guardrails**, not as an immediate permanent policy. The evidence suggests that your specific configuration (senior engineers, no client deadlines, SaaS product model) is nearly ideal for such an experiment. However, the financial reality of $3M ARR supporting 25 senior engineers creates a narrow margin for error. Buffer's experience — a 20% happiness gain followed by a sustainability-driven retrenchment — serves as the most directly relevant precedent and demands caution, not abandonment, of the proposition.

---

## Key Observations

- **The missing comparator:** The Fact Base contains no data on 4-day workweek outcomes specific to B2B SaaS companies of your size and revenue profile. Buffer (a social media SaaS) is the closest parallel, yet even its experience was mixed. This absence is itself significant: if the model were unambiguously successful for SaaS, we would expect more published case studies.

- **The 92% continuation rate conceals selection bias.** All 33 companies in the UK trial volunteered for a 4-day week. They were self-selected enthusiasts. A 92% continuation rate among believers tells us less about general applicability than it appears to.

- **The 40% productivity figure demands scrutiny.** The Microsoft Japan experiment (confidence 0.8) occurred in a unique context: a Japanese corporate culture with extreme overtime norms, a short trial window, and productivity gains partly attributable to meeting reduction and process efficiency — improvements achievable *without* a schedule change.

- **$3M ARR for 25 senior engineers means approximately $120,000 revenue per employee.** If senior engineers command $160,000–$200,000 in total compensation, your gross margin is thin or negative on a per-engineer basis. This is the single most important overlooked detail.

## Evidence Chain

**Step 1 — Assess the fitness of your specific circumstances against the known evidence.**

Your company possesses nearly all the enabling factors identified in successful 4-day week adoptions:
- Knowledge work (SaaS) — cited in the 4 Day Week Global trial as well-suited
- Senior, autonomous engineers — reduce the need for managerial coordination
- No external client deadlines — eliminates the primary critique from HBR (coverage gaps for client-facing businesses)

**Step 2 — Identify the critical risk factor.**

The HBR critique (confidence 0.7) warns that compressed schedules increase burnout risk if total hours are not proportionally reduced. For your senior engineers, a 4-day week that still demands 40 hours of output across 4 days would mean 10-hour days. Among senior knowledge workers, this is unsustainable. Buffer's mid-course correction was driven by exactly this "sustainability concern."

**Step 3 — Weigh the Buffer precedent.**

Buffer's experience is the most relevant data point. A 20% happiness increase (confidence 0.8) is substantial, but the fact that Buffer *adjusted* the policy afterward should not be dismissed as failure. It suggests that the *initial implementation* matters more than the binary decision of 4-day vs. 5-day. Buffer likely discovered that some teams needed to retain 5-day flexibility.

**Step 4 — Consider the financial dimension your query omits.**

At $3M ARR with 25 senior engineers, your payroll-to-revenue ratio is concerning. A 4-day week experiment that reduces output by even 10% could be financially damaging. Conversely, if it improves retention of senior engineers (who are expensive to replace), it could be net positive. The Fact Base does not include data on retention effects, which is a significant blind spot.

**Step 5 — Synthesize a conditional recommendation.**

The chain closes on a conditional "yes" rather than an unconditional one. The evidence supports a trial under these specific conditions:
1. Hours *must* be reduced to 32, not compressed into 4 10-hour days
2. Customer coverage must be explicitly planned for the fifth day (rotating on-call)
3. The trial must include objective productivity metrics, not just happiness surveys
4. A 6-month trial with a pre-defined off-ramp if revenue per employee declines

---

## Explicit Assumptions

- **Revenue stability assumption:** I assume your $3M ARR is relatively stable and not in a high-growth phase where every hour of engineering output compounds. If you were growing rapidly, the opportunity cost of reduced hours would be higher.

- **Senior engineer retention value assumption:** I assume senior engineers at your company are difficult to replace and that improved retention would offset productivity losses. This is a reasonable inference but not in the Fact Base.

- **Customer tolerance assumption:** I assume your SaaS product does not require 5-day support coverage. If customers expect same-day responses on Fridays, this assumption breaks.

- **Self-selection assumption:** I assume a 4-day week would be universally popular among your team. In practice, some senior engineers may prefer to work 5 lighter days rather than 4 heavier ones. The Buffer experience suggests individual preference variation matters.

---

## Blind Spot Acknowledgment

I freely acknowledge the limitations of my method in this analysis:

**Emotional and cultural dimensions.** I have deliberately set aside the morale-boosting, signaling, and recruitment effects of a 4-day week. A 4-day workweek could serve as a powerful differentiator in hiring senior engineering talent — a factor that may outweigh any productivity concerns. A more human observer would note that working one fewer day per week is not merely a schedule change but a statement about values, and that such statements can generate loyalty and cultural cohesion that no spreadsheet can capture.

**Individual variation.** Not all senior engineers work the same way. Some produce their best work in 30 focused hours; others need 50. A rigid policy either way will impose costs on one group. I have treated "senior engineers" as a homogeneous category when they are anything but.

**Missing data.** I would have preferred data on:
- Employee retention rates under 4-day week policies
- Revenue per employee changes before and after adoption at comparable SaaS companies
- The specific structure of Buffer's "adjustment" — what exactly changed?
- Whether the 92% continuation rate includes companies that modified the policy or only those that kept it unchanged

These gaps mean my conclusion is a "likely" rather than a "certain." I present it as such.
